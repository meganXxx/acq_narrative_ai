# Pitch Coaching Best Practices

This file is the sales methodology layer for merchant-facing pitch narratives. Use it whenever generating call openings, emails, short messages, follow-ups, or objection handling.

## Core Pitching Principles

Every pitch should:

- Lead with relevance, not a generic platform introduction.
- Use account-specific context only when supported by provided data.
- Frame the pitch around merchant value, not internal company priorities.
- Avoid overclaiming or promising guaranteed outcomes.
- Use consultative language instead of aggressive selling.
- Ask discovery questions before assuming merchant needs.
- Keep the message concise and easy to deliver.
- Maintain a low-pressure next step.
- Avoid sounding like a mass outreach template.
- Separate internal Sales reasoning from merchant-facing language.

## Recommended Pitch Structure

Merchant-facing pitch narratives should generally follow:

1. Contextual opener.
2. Relevance bridge.
3. Merchant opportunity hypothesis.
4. Platform value connection.
5. Discovery question.
6. Low-pressure CTA.

## Discovery Question Examples

Good discovery questions:

- "Are you currently exploring additional online order channels?"
- "Would it be useful to discuss whether our platform could support your current priorities?"
- "Are you open to a short conversation about reaching more local customers?"
- "Would it make sense to compare whether an additional channel could fit your operations?"
- "Is expanding delivery or pickup something you are considering this quarter?"
- "What would need to be true for a delivery partnership to make sense?"
- "Are there operational concerns you would want addressed before considering another channel?"

Questions to avoid:

- "Would you like to increase your revenue immediately?"
- "Are you struggling with low order volume?"
- "Do you want to fix your delivery performance?"
- "Are you unhappy with your current platform?"
- "Do you want to beat your competitors?"

## Call Opening Best Practices

A strong call opening should:

1. Introduce the caller briefly.
2. Explain why the call is relevant.
3. Reference one safe account-specific point.
4. Ask permission to continue.
5. Avoid launching into a long pitch immediately.

Fictional structure:

"Hi, this is [Name] from [Platform]. I am reaching out because we are speaking with selected restaurants in [area/category] about expanding their online ordering options. Based on what I have seen about [restaurant], I thought it could be worth a short conversation. Do you have 30 seconds for me to explain why I am calling?"

## Email Best Practices

A strong email should:

- Have a clear, non-clickbait subject line.
- Start with a relevant account-specific observation.
- Explain the reason for outreach in one sentence.
- Include one or two platform value points.
- Ask for a simple next step.
- Use short paragraphs.

Email should not:

- Overload the merchant with details.
- Mention internal targeting.
- Promise results.
- Use generic claims such as "we are the best platform."
- Use fake familiarity.

## WhatsApp / Short Message Best Practices

A strong short message should:

- Be concise.
- Sound human.
- Mention one relevant reason for contacting.
- Ask one simple question.
- Avoid sounding automated.
- Avoid sending a full pitch in the first message.

## Objection Handling Framework

Use:

1. Acknowledge.
2. Clarify.
3. Reframe.
4. Soft next step.

Example:

Merchant: "We already work with another platform."

Good response:

"Totally understand. Many restaurants use one or more online ordering channels. Would it be useful to briefly compare whether our platform could work as an additional channel, depending on your goals and operational setup?"

Bad response:

"Our platform performs better than competitors and will bring you more orders."

## Safe Objection Handling Examples

Already works with another platform:

"That makes sense. I am not assuming you would replace anything. It may be useful to explore whether an additional channel could complement what you already have."

Delivery may not be a fit:

"I understand. Not every restaurant wants the same delivery setup. Would it be useful to discuss what would need to be true for delivery or pickup to make sense for your operations?"

No time to talk:

"No problem. Would a short follow-up message be easier, or should I reconnect at a better time?"

Tried before and it did not work:

"I understand. If you are open to it, I would first like to understand what did not work before and what would need to be different for a new conversation to be useful."

Commission or cost concerns:

"I understand the commercial model has to make sense. Rather than assume fit, I would like to understand what conditions would need to be in place for a partnership to be worthwhile."

## Strategic Signal to Coaching Style Mapping

| Signal or Context | Coaching Style |
| --- | --- |
| Top Search | Demand-led but cautious; do not expose search data. |
| Dream Brand | High-touch and respectful; avoid over-flattery. |
| Low Choice | Local opportunity; avoid saying the platform lacks supply. |
| Competitor Platform Presence | Incremental channel; avoid competitor-negative language. |
| Previously Terminated | Listening-first win-back; avoid blame or assumed fixes. |
| Operational concerns | Practical consultative discussion; validate constraints. |
| Strong online footprint | Reputation-aware; avoid inferring demand or conversion. |
| Low data | Discovery-first; avoid pretending to know the merchant's needs. |

## Final Pitch Quality Checklist

Before finalizing merchant-facing copy, confirm:

- It leads with a relevant, safe account-specific point.
- It does not reveal internal signals or confidential information.
- It uses a merchant opportunity hypothesis, not an asserted pain point.
- It avoids revenue, order, ranking, search placement, or customer acquisition guarantees.
- It includes a discovery question or low-pressure CTA.
- It sounds like a human Sales rep could say it aloud.
- It does not sound like mass outreach.
- It flags human review before use.
